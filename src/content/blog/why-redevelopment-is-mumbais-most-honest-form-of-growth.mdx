Mumbai is a city that has run out of space. Not literally — there is land here. But the land that is available is already occupied. It has buildings from forty, fifty, even sixty years ago. Those buildings were built in a different time, for a different Mumbai. Today, the families living in them deserve better. And that is where redevelopment comes in.

I have been in this business for over twenty-five years. I have seen what happens when redevelopment is done well and what happens when it is done poorly. Done well, it transforms lives. Done poorly, it creates more problems than it solves. So I want to talk about why I believe redevelopment is the most honest way to grow a city like Mumbai.

## The problem with new land

When you build on empty land, everything is easy. You do not have to talk to existing residents. You do not have to deal with old structures. You do not have to manage expectations that have been built over decades. You just buy the land and build.

But in Mumbai, empty land is almost impossible to find. The city is built out. If you want to build something new, you either go far out — to places like Karjat or Panvel — or you redevelop what is already standing.

Going far out has its place. We are doing it ourselves with our Karjat Township project. But for the millions of families who live in the heart of Mumbai, in old buildings that are crumbling around them, redevelopment is the only answer.

## What redevelopment really means

Redevelopment is not just demolition and reconstruction. That is the easy part. Redevelopment is about understanding what a community needs and delivering it on the same piece of land where they have lived for generations.

Take a typical scenario. A society in Vile Parle has a building that is forty years old. The building has six flats. The families living there have been neighbours for decades. Their children grew up together. But the building is in bad shape — the plumbing leaks, the electrical wiring is outdated, and there are cracks in the walls.

Now, these families have a choice. They can either keep living in a deteriorating building, hoping nothing goes wrong, or they can come together for redevelopment.

When we step in as developers, we offer them something simple: we will give you a new home, bigger and better than what you have now, at no cost to you. In return, we get the right to build additional flats on the same land, which we sell to recover our costs and make our profit.

This is a fair exchange. The families get a brand new home without spending a rupee. The developer gets a viable project. The city gets better housing stock. Everyone wins.

## Why some redevelopment projects fail

Not all redevelopment projects succeed. Some fail because the developer does not have the financial strength to complete the project. Some fail because the residents cannot agree among themselves. Some fail because of legal complications.

The most common reason for failure is lack of transparency. When residents do not know what is happening, they get suspicious. They start to worry that the developer is cheating them. And once that trust is broken, it is very hard to repair.

That is why we at SNB Group put transparency at the centre of everything we do. We show residents every document. We explain every clause in the agreement. We give them timelines and we stick to them. We treat them not as customers but as partners.

## The human side of redevelopment

I remember one project we did in Vile Parle. The building had a family that had been living there for over fifty years. The grandmother had raised her children in that flat. She had seen her grandchildren grow up there. The flat was small — maybe 450 square feet — but it held a lifetime of memories.

When we started talking about redevelopment, she was the most hesitant. She was worried that the new flat would not feel like home. She was worried about the disruption during construction. She was worried about whether we would actually deliver what we promised.

We sat down with her. We showed her the plans. We explained exactly what her new home would look like — the size, the layout, the finishes. We answered every question she had. And slowly, she came around.

Today, she lives in a 700-square-foot flat in the same building, on the same land, in the same neighbourhood. Her children still visit. Her grandchildren still come over. Nothing changed except the quality of her home.

That is what redevelopment is about. It is not about making money. It is about giving people a better life on the land they already call home.

## Redevelopment and the city

Beyond the individual families, redevelopment is good for Mumbai as a whole. Old buildings are inefficient. They use more water and electricity per square foot. They are not designed for modern living. They do not have adequate parking or common areas.

When we redevelop, we bring everything up to current standards. The new building is more energy-efficient. It has better amenities. It fits more people comfortably on the same footprint. This is density done right — not squeezing people in, but giving them more space to live well.

The city also benefits from better infrastructure. Redevelopment projects often include contributions to road widening, drainage improvements, and other public amenities. The result is a neighbourhood that works better for everyone.

## The honest approach

I said redevelopment is Mumbai's most honest form of growth. Here is why: because it requires you to be honest. You cannot hide anything from the residents. They live there. They see everything. They talk to each other.

If you cut corners, they will know. If you delay the project, they will hold you accountable. If you try to cheat them, they will take you to court.

This keeps developers honest. It forces us to do our best work. And in the end, that is good for everyone — the residents, the developer, and the city.

Redevelopment is not easy. It takes patience, transparency, and financial strength. But when it is done right, it transforms lives. And that is why I believe it is the most honest way to build Mumbai.
