In my twenty-five years of building homes, I have learned one important lesson: the way families actually live is very different from what architects and designers imagine.

When we sit down to design a new project, we start with a lot of research. We talk to families. We visit their homes. We watch how they move through their spaces. And what we find always surprises us.

## The kitchen is the real centre of the home

In most Indian homes, the kitchen is not just a place for cooking. It is the command centre of the house. The mother of the family spends hours there. Children come in to ask for snacks. The husband stops by for a cup of tea. Conversations happen over the kitchen counter.

But most apartment designs treat the kitchen as a small, closed-off room at the back of the house. They give it just enough space for a stove and a sink, with no thought to how it connects to the rest of the home.

We design our kitchens differently. We make them open to the living and dining areas. We add a breakfast counter where family members can sit and talk while someone is cooking. We make sure there is enough storage for all the spices, utensils, and groceries that an Indian kitchen needs.

This small change transforms how the family uses the home. Suddenly, the kitchen is not a place where one person works alone. It becomes the heart of the home, where the family comes together.

## Where the light lands at 6pm

One of the most important things we consider in our designs is natural light. But we do not just think about how much light a room gets. We think about when it gets that light.

A living room that gets bright morning sun is very different from one that gets soft evening light. A bedroom that faces the afternoon sun will be uncomfortable to sleep in. A study that has good light throughout the day will be used more often.

I have a simple practice. I visit the site at different times of the day. I stand in the space where the living room will be and watch where the light falls. I think about what the family will be doing at that time of day. If the light hits the dining table at 8pm, that is when the family has dinner together. If it hits the reading nook at 5pm, that is when the children come home from school.

This attention to light and time makes a home feel right. You may not notice it consciously, but you feel it. The home just works.

## Storage that makes sense

Indian families accumulate things. It is just our nature. We have utensils from weddings, clothes from festivals, books from school, and souvenirs from trips. All of this needs to be stored somewhere.

But most apartments are designed with minimal storage. They assume you will throw things away or keep them in boxes under the bed.

We design storage differently. We build cupboards into every room. We create spaces for suitcases, for extra bedding, for Diwali decorations, for the things that families actually keep. We make sure there is a place for everything.

This may seem like a small thing, but it makes a huge difference. When a family moves into a home and everything has its place, the home stays organised. The family lives comfortably. They do not feel like they are drowning in stuff.

## The flow of daily life

Every family has a rhythm. In the morning, everyone is rushing to get ready for school and work. In the evening, everyone comes back home and winds down. On weekends, the family spends more time together.

A well-designed home supports this rhythm. The bathroom should be accessible from both the bedroom and the hallway. The kitchen should be near the dining area. The living room should have enough space for the whole family to sit together.

But more than that, the home should have spaces for both togetherness and privacy. A teenager needs a quiet corner to study. The parents need a place to relax after the children go to bed. The grandparents need a space that is easy to move around.

We design with all these needs in mind. Every room is thought through for how it will be used at different times of the day and by different members of the family.

## Designing for the next generation

One thing I have noticed is that families change. Children grow up and move out. Parents get older and need more accessible homes. The way a family uses a home in 2026 will be different from how they use it in 2036.

That is why we design homes that can adapt. We use flexible layouts that can be reconfigured. We make sure doors and corridors are wide enough for a wheelchair. We choose materials that age well and are easy to maintain.

A home should not need to be renovated every ten years. It should be designed to serve the family well for decades. That is the kind of longevity we aim for.

## The real brief

At the end of the day, the real brief for any home comes from the family that will live in it. It is not written on paper. It is in the way a mother moves through her kitchen, the way children run through the living room, the way a father reads the newspaper on Sunday morning.

Our job as designers and builders is to understand that brief and deliver a home that supports it. Not a home that looks good in photographs, but a home that feels good to live in.

That is what we try to do with every project. We design for how families actually live. And that makes all the difference.
