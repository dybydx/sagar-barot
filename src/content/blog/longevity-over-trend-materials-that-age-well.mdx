In the real estate business, most developers think about how a building looks on the day of handover. They choose materials that look great in photographs and impress buyers during site visits. But a building is not judged on handover day. It is judged ten monsoons later, when the paint has faded, the walls have settled, and the weather has done its work.

This is a lesson I learned early in my career. I saw buildings that looked beautiful on day one but started deteriorating within a few years. I also saw buildings that were modest at first but aged gracefully, looking better with each passing year.

The difference is the materials you choose.

## The problem with trend-driven materials

Every few years, there is a new trend in construction materials. One year it is Italian marble. Another year it is exposed concrete. Then it is glass facades, then it is textured paint.

These trends come and go. But a building is meant to last for decades. If you choose materials based on what is fashionable today, your building will look dated tomorrow.

More importantly, trendy materials often do not perform well in Mumbai's climate. Italian marble stains easily. Exposed concrete develops cracks in the monsoon. Glass facades turn the building into a greenhouse. These materials look good in magazines, but they do not work well in a city that gets three months of heavy rain every year.

## What we look for in materials

At SNB Group, we choose materials based on three criteria: durability, maintainability, and timelessness.

Durability means the material can withstand Mumbai's weather — the heat, the humidity, the rain. We use materials that have been tested in this climate for decades. We know how they perform because we have seen them perform.

Maintainability means the material is easy to clean and repair. A home is not a museum. People live in it. Children play in it. Things get dirty and things get damaged. If a material is difficult to maintain, it will look shabby within a few years.

Timelessness means the material does not go out of style. We choose natural stone, good quality wood, and neutral colours that will look good for decades. We avoid anything that is too trendy or too specific to a particular design style.

## Our material choices

For flooring, we prefer natural stone like Kota stone and granite. These stones have been used in Indian buildings for centuries. They are hard, durable, and easy to maintain. They do not stain easily. They do not fade in the sun. And they develop a beautiful patina over time.

We avoid high-gloss polished marble, especially in Indian homes where people wear footwear indoors. The marble scratches easily and needs constant maintenance.

For walls, we use high-quality cement plaster with good paint. We avoid textured paints and wallpapers that are difficult to clean and repair. A simple painted wall, done well, looks clean and elegant for years.

For exteriors, we use stone cladding and good quality brickwork. We avoid glass facades and aluminium composite panels that fade and deteriorate in the sun.

For windows and doors, we use good quality wood and powder-coated aluminium. We avoid UPVC and other materials that become brittle in the sun.

## The economics of quality materials

Some developers argue that good quality materials are too expensive. They say buyers care more about the price of the apartment than the quality of the materials.

I disagree. Yes, good materials cost more upfront. But they save money in the long run. A building that uses cheap materials will need constant repairs. The developer's reputation suffers. The residents are unhappy. And in the end, the building depreciates faster.

A building that uses good materials requires less maintenance. The residents are happier. The building retains its value. And the developer's reputation grows.

This is not just good design. It is good business.

## Lessons from old buildings

Some of the best examples of longevity in construction are old buildings. Walk through any old neighbourhood in Mumbai and you will see buildings that are fifty, sixty, even a hundred years old. They are still standing. They still look good. They still function well.

What did those builders do right? They used materials that were local, natural, and time-tested. They used stone, brick, lime plaster, and wood. They built with care and craftsmanship. They did not cut corners.

We can learn from them. Modern technology has given us new materials and new construction methods. But the principles remain the same. Choose materials that last. Build with care. Think about how the building will look and function in twenty years, not just on day one.

## Practical advice for homebuyers

If you are buying a home and want to make sure it will age well, here are a few things to look for.

Ask about the external finishes. What is the building facade made of? Will it need repainting every few years? Stone and brick last much longer than paint and render.

Check the quality of the bathroom and kitchen fittings. These are the areas that see the most wear. Cheap tiles and fittings will need replacement within a few years. Good quality ones will last for decades.

Look at the windows. Good windows with proper seals make a huge difference in noise, dust, and energy efficiency. They also prevent water leakage during the monsoon.

Ask about the plumbing and electrical systems. These are hidden behind the walls, so you cannot see them. But they are the most important systems in your home. Good quality pipes and wiring will save you from major headaches later.

Finally, look at the common areas. The quality of the lobby, the corridors, and the staircase tells you a lot about the builder's attitude towards longevity. If the common areas are well finished, the builder cares about the long term.
