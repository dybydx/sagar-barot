Taking over a family business is one of the most misunderstood transitions in the business world. People think it is easy — you just step into your father's shoes and keep doing what he did. But anyone who has actually done this knows it is not that simple.

I joined my family's real estate practice in 1998. My father had built a respectable business in Mumbai's western suburbs. He had relationships, reputation, and a track record. On paper, I had everything I needed to succeed.

But a family business is not a manual that you inherit. It is a living, breathing thing made up of relationships, trust, and expectations. And as the next generation, you have to earn your place in that ecosystem. You cannot just claim it.

## The weight of a name

When you carry your family name in business, people have expectations. Suppliers remember you as a child. Employees have seen you grow up. Clients are not sure if you are as capable as your father.

I remember my early days on construction sites. The workers would look at me and wonder if this young man knew what he was doing. The contractors would try to take advantage of my inexperience. The architects would talk over me to my father.

It was frustrating, but it taught me something important: respect cannot be inherited. It has to be earned, one project at a time.

So I started small. I spent time on every site. I learned the craft from the ground up — how concrete is poured, how reinforcements are laid, how plumbing is routed. I made mistakes and I learned from them. Slowly, the same people who doubted me started to trust me.

## The advantage of being second-generation

Here is what people do not understand. Being second-generation is not just a challenge. It is also a tremendous advantage.

First, you have a built-in network. The relationships your father built over decades do not disappear. They are there for you to build on. When I needed advice, I could call people my father had worked with for years. When I needed a supplier, I knew who was reliable. When I needed a partner, I knew who to trust.

Second, you have a reputation to protect. This is a double-edged sword, but mostly it is an advantage. Because my family name is on the line, I am careful about every decision I make. I cannot afford to cut corners or take shortcuts. This discipline makes you a better businessman.

Third, you have the benefit of experience without the burden of ego. I saw my father's successes and his failures. I learned from both without having to go through them myself. I knew which strategies worked and which did not.

## The tension between tradition and change

Every next-generation leader faces the same question: how much do I change?

If you change too little, the business stagnates. If you change too much, you lose what made it special. Finding the right balance is the hardest part of the transition.

When I started, the business was built on personal relationships and word-of-mouth. That worked well for my father's generation. But the market was changing. Clients wanted more transparency. They wanted formal processes. They wanted better documentation.

So I introduced changes — slowly. I brought in better project management systems. I improved our legal documentation. I started investing in marketing and branding. But I kept the core values that my father had built: integrity, reliability, and a personal touch.

The result was a business that had the best of both worlds — the trust and relationships of an old-school firm combined with the professionalism of a modern enterprise.

## Building your own identity

One of the biggest challenges of being second-generation is building your own identity. People will always compare you to your father. They will say, "Your father would have done it this way," or "Your father would never have made that decision."

You cannot let this get to you. You have to be your own leader.

My father was a builder in the truest sense. He was hands-on, involved in every detail, and deeply connected to the community. I am different. I am more focused on systems, processes, and scale. I think about the business differently.

Neither approach is wrong. They are just different. The key is to recognise your own strengths and build on them, rather than trying to be a copy of your predecessor.

## The responsibility of renewal

The most important thing I have learned as a second-generation leader is that my job is not just to preserve what my father built. It is to renew it for the next generation.

That means investing in things that will outlast me. It means building a team that can run the business without me. It means creating systems that will survive any individual. It means mentoring the third generation so they do not have to learn everything the hard way.

When I think about the Karjat Township project — our largest ever at 7.25 lakh square feet — I see it as a project that will carry the family name forward for decades. It is not just a real estate development. It is a statement that the business is not just surviving but thriving under new leadership.

## Advice for the next generation

If you are a second-generation leader reading this, here is my advice. Respect what came before you, but do not be afraid to change things. Earn your place through hard work, not through entitlement. Build relationships on your own merit. And most importantly, remember that you are not just preserving a business. You are renewing it for the people who come after you.

It is not easy. There will be days when you wonder if you are good enough. There will be people who doubt you. But if you stay focused on your craft, stay true to your values, and keep building, you will earn the respect you deserve.

That is the second-generation advantage. Not a free pass, but a head start. What you do with it is up to you.
