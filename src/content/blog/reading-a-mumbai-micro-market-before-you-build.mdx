Mumbai is a city of micro-markets. Two streets apart, the same square foot of land can have a completely different value. The same apartment layout can sell for very different prices depending on which side of the road it is on.

This is not something you learn from books. It is something you learn by walking the streets, talking to people, and paying attention to the small details that make each locality unique.

Over twenty-five years of building in Mumbai, I have developed a method for reading a micro-market before we build. Here is how it works.

## The first thing I look at is the people

Before I look at any data — prices, trends, supply — I look at the people. Who lives in this area? What do they do for a living? How long have they been here? What do they value in a home?

A locality like Vile Parle East is very different from Vile Parle West. One has older bungalows and a settled community. The other has newer buildings and younger families. The needs of these two groups are completely different.

In Vile Parle East, where we have done most of our projects, the residents are typically families who have lived there for generations. They have deep roots in the community. They value space, privacy, and familiarity. They are not looking for a trendy apartment. They are looking for a home that matches the way they have always lived.

In a newer micro-market, the residents might be younger professionals. They value amenities, convenience, and modern design. They want a gym, a swimming pool, and a co-working space. They are willing to compromise on size for location.

Understanding the people tells you what to build.

## The second thing is the infrastructure

Mumbai's infrastructure is constantly changing. A new flyover, a metro line, or a road widening project can transform a micro-market overnight.

I spend a lot of time studying infrastructure plans. I look at where new roads are being built, where metro stations are coming up, and where the city is planning to invest. A locality that is difficult to access today might be well-connected in five years.

For example, when the metro line to the western suburbs was announced, we knew that areas near the planned stations would become more valuable. We positioned our projects to take advantage of this improved connectivity.

But infrastructure is not just about transport. It is also about water supply, drainage, electricity, and sewage. A micro-market with reliable utilities is worth more than one where the water supply is erratic. These are the practical considerations that affect how people live every day.

## The third thing is the existing housing stock

What kind of buildings exist in the area? How old are they? What condition are they in? This tells you a lot about the development potential.

An area with many old buildings is ripe for redevelopment. The residents are likely looking for better homes, and the land value is high enough to make redevelopment viable.

An area with many new buildings might be saturated. There might not be much demand for more supply. The competition might be too high.

We look at the age and quality of existing buildings to understand where the market is heading. If buildings are deteriorating and no one is redeveloping them, that is an opportunity. If buildings are new and well-maintained, the opportunity might lie elsewhere.

## The fourth thing is the social fabric

Every micro-market in Mumbai has its own social character. Some are dominated by a particular community. Some have a strong cultural identity. Some are more cosmopolitan.

You need to understand this social fabric before you build. A project that works in one area might fail in another simply because it does not fit the local culture.

For example, in a traditional neighbourhood, families might prefer larger living and dining areas for entertaining. In a more modern area, they might prefer a smaller living area and a larger master bedroom. These preferences are not random. They are shaped by the social fabric of the locality.

We spend time understanding these preferences. We talk to local residents. We attend community events. We listen to what people say about their neighbourhood and what they wish was different.

## The fifth thing is the price trajectory

Finally, we look at the numbers. How have prices changed in this area over the last five years? What is the price per square foot? How does it compare to neighbouring areas?

But we do not just look at current prices. We look at the trajectory. Is the area appreciating faster or slower than the market average? What is driving the appreciation?

An area that is appreciating steadily is a safe bet. An area that has stagnated might be undervalued — or it might have fundamental problems. An area where prices are rising very fast might be a bubble.

We use this data to make informed decisions about where to invest and what price points to target.

## Putting it all together

Reading a micro-market is not a science. It is more like a craft. You gather data from many sources and then you use your judgement to make a decision.

I have made mistakes. I have built projects that did not sell as quickly as I expected because I misread the market. But over time, I have learned to trust my method.

The key is to be humble. You cannot assume you know a locality just because you have built there before. Every project is different. Every micro-market is different. The only way to get it right is to do the work — walk the streets, talk to people, and pay attention.

That is how you build the right project in the right place. And that is how you create value for everyone involved.
